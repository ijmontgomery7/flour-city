Rochester, New York
The Flour city
The history of early Rochester and the rise and fall of the Flour City
Founding of Rochester
1781 - 1825
The History of Rochester begins with the history of the Genesee Valley. Originally inhabited Seneca Tribe of Native Americans. In 1789 the first mill was constructed a deal for land was made with the Seneca tribe in exchange for mill usage. The geography of the area lent a hand to its growth, fertile lands surround the Genesee, while the river itself has powerful waterfalls. These waterfalls would attract the attention of Col. Nathan Rochester who saw more potential for the Genesee Valley. The Rochester and the Brown families would combine their efforts in 1811 and founded the town that would go on to be Rochester. The newly created Rochesterville (the -ville was dropped soon after) housed a population of 700 people. The areas powerful falls and easy connection to Lake Ontario made shipping products easier because the mills close location to areas suitable to dock ships and rafts.
Rise of the Millling Industry
1825 - 1834
The canal completed in 1825 was easily the largest public works project of the young United States. Its 350 miles of waterways allowed the canal barges to ship goods east-west at fraction of its former prices. The plans for the canal involved a stone aqueduct which carried the canal though the heart of Rochester and across the Genesee river. Rochester would become a central trading spot for years to come. Before the canal the transportation of goods from Albany to Buffalo cost $60 per ton, after the canal it fell to $10. This allowed for the Rochester flour mills to have larger radius they could cost effectively ship to. By 1826 only one year after the canal was build the mills were shipping 202,000 barrels of flour in a year. Flour accounted for 800,000 dollars of canal exports from Rochester 2/3rds of the total value of the city’s exports.
The Flour City
1834 - 1954
Rochester officially became a city in 1834 marking the official start of the “Flour City” The flour city name was given to Rochester in the 1930’s for it becoming worldwide renown for not only flour production but quality. 1837 crop failure in the Baltimore area and tied with the creation of two new mills in Rochester, the city became the largest flour producing city in the US with production around 500,000 barrels annually. Flour production however was growing westward now that rail lines started to connect Ohio to larger ports and the increase of streamliners trading on the great lakes and Mississippi. While Rochester’s production was higher than ever new production centers caused havoc in the prices. The high price in January 1829 was 8.25 a barrel with prices falling as low as 5.60 during the year and 4.50 for a year low in 1831, making the milling industry a risky business to invest in.
Fall of the Milling Industry
1854 - Onward
Rochester milling industry ultimately declined from the expansion of America westward, while Rochester was certainly a booming city, Buffalo and Cleveland were growing faster driving investment in these more western cities. The original Col. Rochester’s son would leave the milling industry and move to California in search of gold. The city of Rochester would ultimately shift to other industries as the city become more industrialized. Even at its peak in the 30’s Rochester’s milling only every consistently employed a max of around 300 people and the city would soon be known for other things. Mt. Hope Nurseries was established in 1840 and would start the path to Rochester next cause of national renown this time as the Flower City. Rochester’s milling industry didn’t completely go bust and would remain the “Flour City” all the way until 1954. by 1951 Rochester produced the 561k barrels of flower the most of any city in the world. But it was soon taken over by Minneapolis after poor wheat crops in the ‘50 reduced mill production below capacity.
